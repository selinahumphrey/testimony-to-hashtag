Section I

Introduction

In the spring of 2024, court documents related to Jeffrey Epstein's decades-long sex trafficking network began entering the public record in earnest — releasing a cascade of names, testimonies, and institutional failures into an already volatile information environment. Their release did not produce the reckoning many had anticipated. Instead, they were absorbed into the same fragmented, algorithmically driven media landscape that had been processing, distorting, and debating sexual violence for years: a landscape in which a CNN investigative report on a coordinated online network of sexual predators could accumulate 62 million views in a single month, in which the hashtag #MeToo could trend and recede and trend again without resolving into structural change, and in which the most viral voices speaking about gender and sexual violence were as likely to be those minimizing it as those naming it.

We are living in a period of profound narrative saturation around sexual violence and profound narrative asymmetry. Sexual assault has never been more present as a subject of public discourse. It surfaces in courtroom testimony and congressional hearings, in investigative journalism and viral social media threads, in celebrity confessions and coordinated harassment campaigns. And yet the voices most directly shaped by the experience of sexual violence — survivors themselves — are frequently crowded out, reframed, or absorbed into narratives not of their own making. The sheer volume of discourse about sexual violence does not guarantee that survivors are heard. It may, in fact, make it harder. For survivors, this has never been a discourse. It has been a fully integrated aspect of their physical and mental lives.

"Let me prove how out of control women have become in our gynocratic society which expects men to do all the work, while having no rights or respect at all. Men. Have you suffered from, or been threatened with a #MeToo accusation?"

Andrew Tate · X (formerly Twitter) · January 7, 2024

This asymmetry is purposeful. Researchers studying online gender discourse have documented the systematic ways in which misogynistic rhetoric — amplified by algorithmic recommendation systems and organized communities of bad-faith actors — functions as a suppressive force, reframing abuse as consequence, coercion as courtship, and survivor testimony as performance or exaggeration. Beyond offense, this rhetoric shapes the epistemic environment in which survivors decide whether to speak, how to speak, and whether they will be believed when they do. It colonizes the same platforms where survivors have attempted to build communities of disclosure and mutual recognition, and it does so with enormous reach and speed.

Where a survivor speaks shapes how they speak. Language does not exist outside of its context.

On September 9, 2016, a twenty-three-year-old woman known to the world only as Emily Doe sat in a California courtroom and began to read. For twelve pages, she addressed the man who had raped her behind a dumpster on the Stanford University campus while she lay unconscious — a man who, six months later, would walk out of that same courthouse sentenced to six months in county jail. Her statement, later published in full, was read by eleven million people in four days. It was read aloud on the floor of the United States Congress. It went viral — a word that does not begin to capture what it means for a survivor's most private devastation to become public property, a rallying cry, a cultural moment.

Chanel Miller — the name Emily Doe would eventually claim as her own — did not choose Twitter. She chose a courtroom, a judge, and twelve pages of carefully constructed language. Her words did not stay there. They migrated, were shared, were screenshot and captioned and circulated until they became something beyond testimony: they became a text that a culture used to reckon with itself.

One year later, in October 2017, actress Alyssa Milano posted a tweet asking anyone who had been sexually harassed or assaulted to reply with the words "Me too." Within twenty-four hours, the phrase had been tweeted nearly half a million times. What many did not know — and what remains an uncomfortable fault line in the movement's history — is that "Me Too" had been the life's work of Tarana Burke since 2006, a Black activist who had built a grassroots movement for survivors of color long before a hashtag gave it a name the mainstream was willing to hear. The viral moment of 2017 did not create survivor speech. It amplified certain kinds of it, in certain voices, on certain platforms — and left others behind.

This project begins with the premise that where a survivor speaks shapes how they speak. A victim impact statement delivered to a federal judge operates under a different set of rhetorical constraints than a tweet composed in the middle of the night or a quote filtered through a journalist constructing a narrative about a network of online sexual predators. Each of these spaces — legal, journalistic, and networked — makes different demands on survivors: what they are allowed to say, how they are expected to feel, what counts as credible, and what gets heard at all. And yet across all three spaces, survivors are doing something recognizable: they are trying to make language do the work that institutions have failed to do. They are trying to be believed.

From Testimony to Hashtag takes up that effort as its object of study. Drawing on three distinct corpora — victim impact statements from the trials of Brock Turner and Larry Nassar, journalistic accounts of survivors of a network-facilitated sexual assault culture as reported by CNN, and a dataset of tweets collected under the hashtags #MeToo and #WhyIDidntReport — this project applies sentiment analysis and computational-linguistic methods to map the emotional and rhetorical contours of survivor language across institutional contexts. Using Python-based sentiment scoring, rolling narrative arc visualization, and mean affect analysis, I construct what I am calling a baseline model: not a definitive account of how survivors speak, but a first, rigorous attempt to let the patterns in the language speak for themselves.

The argument this project makes is both methodological and political. Methodologically, it proposes that computational tools — when applied with feminist critical frameworks and appropriate humility about their limitations — can surface patterns in survivor language that close reading alone cannot reveal. Politically, it insists that centering survivor voice, treating it as structured, analyzable, rhetorically meaningful discourse rather than raw emotion or anecdote, is itself an act of epistemic repair. Miranda Fricker's concept of epistemic injustice — the harm done to someone specifically in their capacity as a knower — haunts every corpus examined here. So does Leigh Gilmore's insistence that we live in a culture that structurally doubts survivors, and Kate Manne's argument that misogyny is less a matter of individual hatred and more one of systemic enforcement. Sentiment analysis cannot measure epistemic injustice directly, but it can show us where language flattens, where it spikes, where survivors reach for anger and where they reach for hope — and in doing so, it can make visible the shape of what it costs to speak.

Section II

Historical & Theoretical Background

The #MeToo Movement in Historical Context

Civil rights activist Tarana Burke created a space for girls and women who have been sexually abused when she founded the Me Too movement. The expression "me too" originated around 1995, when a young girl described her survivor status to Burke, who recalled: "I didn't know how to respond, how to support. I wanted to say something deep and profound… I wanted to tell her this happened to me too." In 2007, Burke organized Just Be, a nonprofit providing resources for victims of sexual assault and harassment — resources she herself had not had when that young girl first confided in her. She called the broader project Me Too. Burke has pointed to Maya Angelou's I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings as foundational inspiration, explaining: "It was the first time I read or heard or experienced anybody else who had experienced sexual abuse."

Burke's movement exploded into mainstream consciousness in October 2017, when Alyssa Milano asked people on Twitter to respond with "me too" if they had been victims of sexual harassment or assault. Within hours, tens of thousands had taken up the #MeToo hashtag. What that viral moment obscured was the decade of grassroots labor Burke had already invested — labor centered on Black women and girls and on communities whose experiences of sexual violence the mainstream had long refused to center. The 2017 iteration amplified certain voices while displacing others, a tension that continues to shape how the movement is remembered and mobilized.

The #MeToo moment did not emerge in a historical vacuum. It belongs to a longer feminist genealogy that includes second-wave speak-outs, the Take Back the Night marches of the 1970s and 1980s, and the passage of the Violence Against Women Act in 1994 — legislation that scholar Stephanie Stupakis has described as "the cornerstone of our nation's response to domestic and sexual violence," filling a critical gap in services for victims of assault, harassment, dating violence, and stalking. The ongoing threat to VAWA's reauthorization represents one of the most concrete political stakes of the cultural battles this project examines.

Feminist Scholarship on Survivor Language and Testimony

When a survivor speaks — in a courtroom, to a journalist, or into the void of a social media platform — they are performing credibility in a system that has historically withheld it from them. Miranda Fricker's concept of epistemic injustice names precisely this harm: the wrong done to someone specifically in their capacity as a knower, when their account is not taken seriously because of who they are rather than what they say. Leigh Gilmore extends this analysis in Tainted Witness, arguing that our culture has built structural mechanisms for doubting survivors — mechanisms that activate most reliably when they testify about violence done to them by people with power.

The rhetorical constraints placed on survivors in legal contexts are particularly acute. A victim impact statement is addressed to a judge, delivered in public, and often represents the one moment in the legal process where a survivor is permitted to speak in the first person. And yet that speech is shaped by institutional expectations: it should demonstrate harm without appearing unhinged, grief without appearing vindictive, credibility without appearing rehearsed. Kate Manne's framework helps explain why — misogyny is less a matter of individual hatred and more a one of systemic enforcement.

Digital platforms have opened new spaces for survivor testimony, with their own affordances and constraints. Social media offers anonymity, reach, and the organizing power of the hashtag — but it also exposes survivors to coordinated harassment, algorithmic suppression, and the parasocial intimacy of speaking to an unknown audience. Sara Ahmed's concept of feminist killjoys helps illuminate the position survivors occupy in these spaces: the one who names violence is the one who disrupts the peace and is punished accordingly.

The Most Silenced: Trans Survivors in the Current Political Climate

Any honest accounting of who gets to speak — and who is structurally prevented from speaking — requires naming the population for whom the cost of testimony is highest and the institutional support most absent. Trans people, and trans women in particular, experience sexual violence at rates that dwarf those of the general population. Studies consistently find that between 47% and 66% of trans individuals have experienced sexual assault. Trans women of color face rates even higher. And yet trans survivors are among the least represented in legal proceedings, journalistic accounts, and public advocacy movements. They are not simply underrepresented in this corpus — they are, in most institutional spaces, effectively excluded from it.

The reasons are structural and compounding. Legal systems that do not recognize a survivor's gender identity expose them to repeated misgendering and delegitimization during testimony — the very institution meant to hear them becomes another site of harm. Journalistic coverage of trans survivors, where it exists at all, frequently centers the trans identity over the assault, sensationalizing rather than witnessing. On social media, where the #MeToo movement has found some of its most powerful expressions, trans survivors who speak publicly face not only the harassment directed at all survivors but the specific, organized violence of anti-trans campaigns that have intensified dramatically in the current political moment.

Across the United States, the United Kingdom, and much of the world, trans rights have become a primary site of legislative and cultural contest. In the U.S., hundreds of bills restricting trans existence — in healthcare, in education, in public space — have been introduced or passed since 2020. In the UK, the ongoing debate over gender recognition reform has produced an environment in which trans people's credibility and legitimacy are publicly contested daily. In this climate, a trans survivor who speaks publicly about sexual violence does not simply risk disbelief — they risk becoming a target for the same political machinery that has spent years insisting their identity itself is a fiction. The calculus of silence for trans survivors is political, systemic, and reinforced by the current moment in ways that make speaking genuinely dangerous in ways cis survivors do not face in the same configuration.

This project does not include trans survivor testimony in its corpus — not because that testimony is absent, but because collecting it responsibly would require a dedicated methodological and ethical framework that exceeds the scope of this baseline model. The #MeToo movement has been critiqued, including by trans advocates, for centering cis women's experiences as the default of survivor speech. That critique is valid, and it is a limitation this project inherits and acknowledges. It is worth noting that the gender identity of users in the Twitter/X corpus cannot be determined — the dataset is anonymous, and the posts very well may include survivors of all gender identities, including trans people. That possibility does not resolve the gap; it names it differently. Because gender identity is not recoverable from the data, trans voices that are present in the corpus are invisible as such, absorbed into a collective that treats gender as assumed rather than examined. This is itself a form of the erasure the section is trying to name. Future work must develop methodologies that can hold trans testimony on its own terms — not dissolved into aggregate patterns but read with the specificity those voices deserve. The silence of trans survivors as a named, visible population in this corpus is not neutral. It reflects exactly the epistemic injustice this project is trying to name: the gap between who experiences violence and who gets to testify to it on terms the culture will receive.

Using computational tools to study survivor language requires methodological and ethical justification. Lauren Klein and Catherine D'Ignazio's Data Feminism provides the essential framework: data is never neutral, and the choice to collect, analyze, and visualize it is always already a political act. Framing this project within feminist praxis means acknowledging that VADER, pandas, and Voyant Tools are not objective instruments — tools are built by people with assumptions, trained on corpora with biases, and deployed in contexts with stakes. Naming those conditions is a core methodological commitment.

Ted Underwood's Distant Horizons offers a model for how computational pattern recognition can complement rather than replace close reading, revealing structures that are invisible at the sentence level but legible at scale. Safiya Umoja Noble's Algorithms of Oppression provides the necessary counterweight: a reminder that NLP systems trained on majority-culture data encode and reproduce the biases of that culture, which has historically worked to minimize and disbelieve survivor testimony. This project positions itself at the intersection of these two traditions — using computational tools with humanistic humility and treating the gaps and failures of the tools as data in themselves.

Section III

Methodology

All sentiment analysis was conducted using Python 3 in JupyterLab via Anaconda, an open-source distribution widely used in computational social science and digital humanities research. The analysis relied on two primary libraries: pandas for data ingestion and manipulation, and the Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK) for linguistic processing and sentiment scoring.

Sentiment scores were generated using VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner), a lexicon- and rule-based sentiment analysis tool developed by Hutto and Gilbert (2014) and accessed through NLTK. VADER was selected because it was specifically designed to handle short, informal texts — including social media posts — and is sensitive to capitalization, punctuation, and intensifiers. It produces a compound score ranging from -1 (maximally negative) to +1 (maximally positive), which served as the primary unit of analysis throughout this project. For the long-form texts, each document was sentence-tokenized using NLTK's sent_tokenize function, with each sentence assigned a sequential number to preserve narrative order. VADER was then applied sentence by sentence, and rolling window averages were plotted using Matplotlib to visualize the emotional arc of each text over time.

Twitter/X data was collected using Zeeschuimer, a browser extension developed by the Digital Methods Initiative (DMI) that captures social media data directly from the platform's native interface as the researcher scrolls, bypassing API restrictions. This method was made necessary by Twitter/X's decision to severely restrict and monetize API access. Posts were filtered using the hashtags #MeToo and #WhyIDidntReport, yielding a corpus snapshot rather than an exhaustive archive — a limitation acknowledged throughout this project.

Additional corpus analysis was conducted in Voyant Tools, an open-source web-based text analysis environment (Sinclair and Rockwell, 2016). The full Twitter/X corpus was uploaded to Voyant, where high-frequency term distribution was visualized using the built-in word cloud tool, and semantic word relationships were mapped using the Constellation visualization — a network graph viewer that explores Word2Vec models to represent how words cluster and co-occur across the corpus. VADER and Voyant Tools provide complementary lenses: VADER quantifies emotional valence at the sentence and document level, while Voyant surfaces the thematic and relational structure of language across the corpus as a whole.

Note on Instagram data — Data collection was attempted on Instagram using the same Zeeschuimer methodology. However, Instagram content did not yield analyzable text — posts did not transfer coherently into Voyant Tools or the VADER pipeline. In the interest of methodological consistency, written Instagram content was excluded from computational analysis. Instagram's equivalent of Twitter/X text content increasingly lives on Threads, Meta's text-based platform. Future work could productively compare Threads content alongside the Twitter/X corpus collected here. A small number of impactful images were hand-collected from public Instagram accounts and are presented as visual rhetoric in the supplement below.
Note on the Epstein files — Epstein survivor testimonies were not included in this project. Because the case remains an active legal situation, transcripts have not reached a stable, ethically appropriate form for computational analysis. Their inclusion in future iterations of this model is both imminent and necessary.
Note on limitations — VADER was trained on general and social media language. Emotional restraint — a common and deliberate rhetorical strategy in legal testimony, where displays of feeling can be weaponized against a survivor's credibility — may register as neutrality in VADER scoring rather than as the significant labor it represents. Findings should be understood as measuring surface linguistic affect. See the Discussion section for a full treatment.
Section IV — Platform One

Journalistic Language: The CNN Investigation

Published on March 26, 2026, CNN's As Equals investigation — "Exposing a Global 'Online Rape Academy' That Is Teaching Men How to Abuse Women and Evade Detection" — documented a coordinated, international network of men using private Telegram groups and mainstream pornography platforms to share tactics for drugging and sexually assaulting their partners, record the abuse, and circulate it as content. The platform at the center of the investigation, Motherless.com, logged approximately 62 million visits in February 2026 alone and hosted more than 20,000 user-uploaded videos tagged as "sleep" content — a euphemism for footage of women who are unconscious or sedated. The investigation drew on months of undercover reporting, expert testimony, platform analysis, and the accounts of three survivors who agreed to speak publicly.

The investigation's title borrows a phrase from Sandrine Josso, a French lawmaker who was herself drugged by a former French senator and has since campaigned to raise awareness about drug-facilitated sexual assault. Josso described these online communities as "schools of violence" — places where, she said, "every subject is taught. There are all the 'subjects' and 'disciplines' needed to become a good rapist or sexual predator." Psychologist Annabelle Montagne, who assessed half of the men convicted in the Dominique Pelicot mass rape trial, told CNN that participants in these communities find a sense of "brotherhood" that meets and reinforces their "narcissistic" needs — a collective normalization of violence that makes individual accountability harder to enforce and harder to name.

The Journalistic Frame

When a survivor speaks to a reporter rather than to a judge or into a hashtag, their language passes through an institutional filter. Journalistic conventions — objectivity, sourcing requirements, narrative compression, the demand for a legible story arc — shape what gets said, what gets quoted, and what gets cut. The survivor is present, but present through a frame they did not build. The CNN investigation handles this tension carefully: survivor accounts are foregrounded, named (or pseudonymized at the survivor's request), and attributed with explicit editorial acknowledgment of the vulnerability involved. And yet the structure of investigative journalism still necessarily mediates: quotes are selected, sequenced, and surrounded by reporter-authored context that shapes how they land.

This mediation leaves linguistic traces that sentiment analysis can help surface. Survivor speech in journalistic contexts tends toward compression — the full arc of experience must fit the logic of the story, the paragraph, the pull quote. Hedged language, present-tense reflection, and the grammar of ongoing trauma ("it's always right there beside you") appear alongside declarative statements of fact and legal outcome. The emotional register is neither the controlled formality of the courtroom nor the raw immediacy of the hashtag. Journalistic survivor speech occupies its own rhetorical space — and the three accounts below make that space visible.

The Survivors: In Their Own Words

Three survivors consented to speak with CNN for this investigation, each having experienced drug-facilitated intimate partner sexual assault. Their accounts are presented here as primary corpus material — survivor language operating within the journalistic frame, carrying the full weight of testimony while shaped by the conventions of reported speech.

Zoe Watts — Devon, England

Zoe Watts learned on an otherwise ordinary Sunday in 2018 — after returning from church with her husband of sixteen years — that he had been crushing their son's sleeping medication into her nightly cup of tea and raping her while she was unconscious. He confessed in a list she described as delivered "as if it was, you know, a shopping list." The admission shattered the safety she had assumed in her own home. "We worry about who's coming behind us, walking down the street," she said. "We worry about going to our car late at night in a car park, but we don't worry about who you lie next to. I didn't realize I had to." The legal process that followed took four years, saw her children targeted for bullying at school, and left her social network all but destroyed. Her former husband told police he believed she had wanted to wake up to find him having sex with her — a consent defense that echoes the argument made by several of Dominique Pelicot's co-defendants in France. He is now serving an eleven-year sentence for rape, sexual assault by penetration, and drugging. Watts still struggles with the word rape to describe what happened. "There's a shame and a guilt that comes with it," she said. "Maybe I should have known. Why didn't I connect those dots?"

Amanda Stanhope — Wigan, England

Amanda Stanhope was inspired to speak publicly by Gisèle Pelicot's declaration at her former husband's trial that "shame must change sides." Over a five-year period, Stanhope would fall asleep without remembering how and wake to bruises, different clothes, no memory of why. After waking to her partner raping her and asking him to stop, she was gaslit. "He would say, 'well, you're on too much medication. You must be imagining it. That didn't happen. You're mental. You're crazy,'" she recalled. "He was trying to change my reality." When she eventually went to police — with the support of her brother — the response compounded the harm. Presented with video evidence of the assault, officers told her it couldn't be used because it "looks like you're pretending to be asleep." Her former partner was eventually charged with rape and sexual assault, but took his own life before the case went to court. Stanhope now posts on social media to raise awareness and encourage other survivors to trust their instincts. The trauma has fundamentally reshaped how she moves through the world. "I see everyone as a potential predator," she said. "It took my innocence for people away."

Valentina — Northern Italy

Valentina, a mother of two who asked CNN not to use her real name, discovered videos her husband of twenty years had filmed documenting his abuse of her after drugging her with alcohol and sedatives. The discovery reoriented her understanding of two decades of marriage. "I can't conceive of the fact that a woman could be treated like slaughterhouse meat," she said. "Because in the end, that's what I was." She does not remember the abuse — but its residue is constant and involuntary. Triggers are mundane: a bed, a camera, a different scent. "No matter how much you try to brush it off," she said, "it's always right there beside you." She noted she was fortunate to find the videos at all — without them, she said, it "would have been a bit… difficult to believe, because I had no marks." Her former husband was sentenced to eight years in prison for multiple aggravated sexual assaults in 2021. Years later, she is still working with a psychologist, still working to understand his motivation. "As much as I might be happy, smiling, on the outside, then I come home," she said. "I still have to deal… make friends with my nightmares, which are always just behind the door."

Section V — Platform Two

Legal Language: Victim Impact Statements

A victim impact statement is, legally speaking, the survivor's opportunity to address the court directly before sentencing — to put on record, in their own words, what was done to them and what it cost. It is often the only moment in the entire legal process in which they are permitted to speak in the first person. And yet that speech is profoundly constrained: it is addressed to a judge, it is performed in public, it is subject to time limits and decorum expectations, and it exists within a system that has already determined guilt and is now in the process of determining consequence. The survivor speaks, but into an institution that has its own ideas about what they should say and how they should say it.

Linda Alcoff and Laura Gray have theorized the ways in which survivor discourse is both potentially transgressive and potentially recuperable — the institution can absorb a survivor's testimony, use it as evidence of harm, and still return a verdict or a sentence that reaffirms the system's fundamental indifference to the scope of that harm. The four statements examined here — from Chanel Miller, Aly Raisman, Kyle Stephens, and Rachael Denhollander — all navigated this paradox, each in a distinct register.

Chanel Miller

Chanel Miller delivered her statement on September 2, 2016 at the sentencing of Brock Turner, convicted of three counts of felony sexual assault for attacking her while she was unconscious at a Stanford University fraternity party. Turner was sentenced to six months in county jail — a sentence that became a national flashpoint. Miller's statement, initially published anonymously by BuzzFeed News, was read by eleven million people in four days, entered the congressional record, and stands as one of the most widely read documents in the recent history of survivor advocacy. She later reclaimed her identity publicly in her memoir, Know My Name.

The statement opens with one of the most arresting single lines in this entire corpus: "You don't know me, but you've been inside me, and that's why we're here today." That sentence establishes everything that follows — the intimacy of violation from a stranger, the clinical fact of penetration as the grounds for standing in a courtroom, and the refusal to soften what happened into something more legible to an institution that would spend the next year doing exactly that. It is worth noting that this line does not appear in the version of Miller's statement reproduced in The American Yawp Reader, a widely used open-access primary source collection taught in university American history courses across the country. Its excision from an educational text is not incidental — it is a curatorial decision that removes the statement's most visceral and unambiguous sentence, the one that most directly refuses euphemism. That a document about sexual violence should be made more palatable for institutional consumption by cutting its most confrontational line is itself an act of the same silencing the statement names. The version most students encounter has already been edited on their behalf.

What follows is a reconstruction of a night she cannot remember, assembled from evidence — from what nurses told her, from pine needles in her hair, from the absence of her underwear when she reached for it in a hospital bathroom. "I still remember the feeling of my hands touching my skin and grabbing nothing." The detail is devastating precisely because it is physical, specific, and almost quiet. Then: "everything inside me was silenced. I still don't have words for that feeling." These two sentences sit at the rhetorical center of the statement's first movement — not rage, not accusation, but a woman trying to stay conscious inside a body that has become a crime scene.

She describes the examination that followed: nurses photographing abrasions, swabs inserted, a Nikon aimed between her legs, her vagina smeared with blue paint to test for injury. And then, after hours of this: "I stood there examining my body beneath the stream of water and decided, I don't want my body anymore. I was terrified of it, I didn't know what had been in it, if it had been contaminated, who had touched it. I wanted to take off my body like a jacket and leave it at the hospital with everything else." That image — the body as something removable, something that could be left behind — is among the most arresting in any survivor text this project examined. VADER will read this passage as highly negative. What the score cannot capture is the terrible logic of it: the body as burden, as evidence, as the one thing that cannot be walked away from.

Miller learned the details of her own assault by reading a news article at her desk at work. "I learned what happened to me the same time everyone else in the world learned what happened to me." The violation of that — not the assault itself but the epistemic humiliation of discovering what was done to you in a news brief — is followed immediately by the detail that has since become emblematic of what the statement indicts: "At the bottom of the article, after I learned about the graphic details of my own sexual assault, the article listed his swimming times." Her response is controlled fury: "Throw in my mile time if that's what we're doing. I'm good at cooking, put that in there, I think the end is where you list your extra-curriculars to cancel out all the sickening things that've happened."

The statement does not stay in the past. What follows the account of the assault itself is the account of the legal process — and this is where the statement becomes something else entirely. Miller was warned that because she could not remember, the defendant would get to write the script. "He can say whatever he wants and no one can contest it. I had no power, I had no voice, I was defenseless. My memory loss would be used against me." That warning proved accurate. At trial, a new story emerged — of kissing and dancing, of consent granted in three words before she was on the ground. Miller's response to the consent claim is precise and withering: "Future reference, if you are confused about whether a girl can consent, see if she can speak an entire sentence. You couldn't even do that. Just one coherent string of words. If she can't do that, then no. Don't touch her, just no."

Then comes what may be the single most formally significant passage in this corpus. Miller reproduces, verbatim and without commentary, the questions she was asked at trial:

How old are you? How much do you weigh? What did you eat that day? Well what did you have for dinner? Who made dinner? Did you drink with dinner? No, not even water? When did you drink? How much did you drink? What container did you drink out of? Who gave you the drink? How much do you usually drink? Who dropped you off at this party? At what time? But where exactly? What were you wearing? Why were you going to this party? What'd you do when you got there? Are you sure you did that? But what time did you do that? What does this text mean? Who were you texting? When did you urinate? Where did you urinate? With whom did you urinate outside? Was your phone on silent when your sister called? Do you remember silencing it? Really because on page 53 I'd like to point out that you said it was set to ring. Did you drink in college? You said you were a party animal? How many times did you black out? Did you party at frats? Are you serious with your boyfriend? Are you sexually active with him? When did you start dating? Would you ever cheat? Do you have a history of cheating? What do you mean when you said you wanted to reward him? Do you remember what time you woke up? Were you wearing your cardigan? What color was your cardigan? Do you remember any more from that night? No? Okay, we'll let Brock fill it in.

There is no analytical gloss needed here. The list enacts the experience it describes — the pummeling, the accumulation, the narrowing, the final handoff to the person who assaulted her. It is the most formally controlled moment in the statement, and one of the most formally controlled moments in any survivor document in this corpus. VADER will score each question as near-neutral. The passage as a whole is anything but. It is the legal system's violence against survivors made visible in syntax.

Miller reconstructs the trial's verdict on institutional proportionality in two of the statement's most quoted lines: "The probation officer weighed the fact that he has surrendered a hard earned swimming scholarship. If I had been sexually assaulted by an un-athletic guy from a community college, what would his sentence be?" And on the six-month sentence itself, the most controlled sentence in the document: "It felt serious. That's all I'm going to say."

The statement closes not with bitterness but with something harder to name — a determined, outward-facing solidarity that contains the whole of what came before it. "To girls everywhere, I am with you. On nights when you feel alone, I am with you. When people doubt you or dismiss you, I am with you. I fought everyday for you. So never stop fighting, I believe you." And then the lighthouse passage, which has been quoted and requoted since 2016, but which earns its resonance from everything that preceded it: "Lighthouses don't go running all over an island looking for boats to save; they just stand there shining." The movement from a woman who wanted to leave her body at a hospital to a woman who has become a lighthouse — that is the arc this statement traces, and it is why eleven million people read it in four days.

Aly Raisman

Aly Raisman delivered her statement in January 2018 at the sentencing of Larry Nassar. A two-time Olympic champion, Raisman had been abused by Nassar beginning when she was a teenager, during treatment sessions she was required to attend. The statement is among the most institutionally focused of the Nassar proceedings — but it does not begin there. It begins in the room, addressed directly to Nassar: "Larry, you do realize now that we, this group of women you so heartlessly abused over such a long period of time, are now a force and you are nothing." The syntax of that sentence is worth examining: "we… are now a force" placed against "you are nothing" — the inversion is absolute, and it arrives in the statement's opening before any context has been established. This is not a statement that builds toward its indictment. It opens with the verdict already delivered.

The rhetorical signature of Raisman's statement is its refusal to let institutions hide behind language. She quotes USA Gymnastics CEO Kerry Perry's published statement — "their powerful voices leave an indelible imprint on me" — and responds with a sentence whose flatness is its force: "at this point, talk is cheap." Four words after a paragraph of institutional rhetoric. The contrast is the argument. She catalogs the gap between what the organizations claimed and what they permitted: "I cringe to think that your influence remains in the policies that are supposed to keep athletes safe." The word "cringe" is doing unusual work here — it is a physical, involuntary response embedded in what reads otherwise as formal address, a moment where the body's reaction breaks through the statement's controlled register.

VADER will register the statement's institutional passages — the cataloging of USA Gymnastics' failures, the questioning of the USOC's silence — at a different affective register than its direct address to Nassar, which spikes in both negative and positive directions: negative when naming what was done, positive in the declarations of collective strength. The statement operates on two syntactic tracks simultaneously. Sentences addressed to Nassar are short, declarative, physical. Sentences addressed to the court and the organizations are longer, subordinated, procedural. That split is a deliberate rhetorical architecture, holding personal and institutional accountability in two different grammars at once. The closing line collapses both: "Abusers, your time is up." Three words. Subject, possessive, predicate. The shortest possible sentence that contains everything.

Kyle Stephens

Kyle Stephens was the first survivor to testify at the Nassar sentencing, and she did so having spent years fighting to be believed by the people closest to her. Nassar had been a family friend; the abuse began when she was six years old, in the basement of his home, with both families present upstairs. When she disclosed the abuse to her parents at twelve, they chose to believe Nassar. Her father's disbelief shaped everything — their relationship, her sense of reality, her relationship with her own memory — until his death by suicide in 2016, a loss Stephens holds Nassar accountable for directly: "Larry Nassar wedged himself between myself and my family and used his leverage as my parents' trusted friend to pry us apart until we fractured. And fractured we did." That final sentence — three words, a period — is a syntactic mirror of the fracture it names.

Stephens's statement is formally distinctive for what it does with the body. Where Chanel Miller reconstructs hers from evidence gathered after the fact, Stephens names each act done to her body in specific anatomical language, then anchors it in a physical memory that survives the decades: "You convinced my parents that you didn't pull my feet into your lap, unzip your pants, and rub your erect penis against my bare skin, but I still flinch when my feet are near someone's lap." The structure — "you convinced my parents… but I still" — repeats across multiple sentences, each iteration setting institutional disbelief against bodily memory. The body remembers what the parents chose not to believe. The syntax makes that argument without stating it.

The statement also contains one of the most formally controlled passages in the legal corpus: the "For me, it was a girl" sequence, in which Stephens translates the clinical language of psychological harm — depression, anxiety, eating disorders — into embodied specificity. "For me, it was a girl crying on the floor for hours trying not to rip out too much of her hair. For me, it was a girl wanting the pain to stop so badly that she woke up for months to the thought, I want to die." The repetition of "For me, it was a girl" performs the dissociation it describes — Stephens speaks of herself in the third person, at a remove, as though the girl and the woman testifying are not quite the same person. VADER will score these sentences at the floor of negativity. What the score cannot capture is the syntactic choice to distance — the grammar of someone who survived by learning to watch herself from outside.

The closing address to Nassar is the statement's rhetorical inversion: "Perhaps you have figured it out by now, but little girls don't stay little forever. They grow into strong women that return to destroy your world." The politeness of "perhaps you have figured it out" preceding a declaration of destruction is the syntax of someone who has spent years mastering the art of saying something devastating in the language of decorum. VADER will read "destroy" and score accordingly. What it cannot read is the decades of precision that produced the sentence.

Rachael Denhollander

Rachael Denhollander was the first person to file a formal police report against Larry Nassar, in 2016, after going on the record with the Indianapolis Star. She had been fifteen when Nassar began abusing her under the guise of medical treatment, with her mother present in the room. Her victim impact statement, delivered in January 2018 as the last of the Nassar proceedings, is the most formally complex document in this corpus — a text that moves across three distinct rhetorical registers: the personal and embodied, the legal and institutional, and the theological. Each register has its own syntax, and part of what makes the statement extraordinary is how deliberately Denhollander moves between them, using the shift in register itself as a rhetorical act.

The institutional register is the statement's backbone. Denhollander documents institutional failure, sentence by sentence, with the precision of a legal brief. She names Kathie Klages, a former Michigan State gymnastics coach, who is alleged to have shown a survivor a reporting form while telling her that filing it could get Nassar in trouble. She quotes MSU officials who told survivors they "didn't understand the nuanced differences between sexual assault and a medical exam." The syntax of that quotation is worth pausing over: the institutional response to assault allegations is itself framed as an educational problem — the survivor simply lacks the sophistication to understand what was done to them. Denhollander reproduces the sentence without editorial comment. The comment is the reproduction.

Throughout, she returns to a single question, repeated with accumulating force: "How much is a little girl worth?" The first time it appears, it is a genuine question addressed to the court. By the third or fourth iteration, the repetition has transformed it into something closer to an indictment — the question the institutions never asked, the one whose absence explains everything that followed. VADER will score each instance of the phrase similarly. What the score cannot register is how repetition in testimony functions differently than repetition in other genres: here it is the sound of someone refusing to let an unanswered question be forgotten.

The theological register appears most fully in the statement's closing direct address to Nassar, and it is worth reading carefully rather than summarizing. Denhollander tells him she forgives him — but the syntax of the forgiveness is not absolution. "I pray you experience the soul-crushing weight of guilt so you may someday experience true repentance and true forgiveness from God, which you need far more than forgiveness from me." The grammar redirects the expected endpoint of forgiveness — toward Nassar's reckoning, not his relief. And then the closing: "I pity you for it." Four words after paragraphs of theological and legal architecture. The statement's longest register — its most elaborated, most sustained — ends in a sentence so short it functions as a door closing. VADER will score "pity" as mildly negative. The statement as a whole has built, across thousands of words, to make those four words land as a verdict.

Section VI — Platform Three

Networked Language: #MeToo & #WhyIDidntReport

Social media platforms offer a fundamentally different rhetorical context for survivor testimony than either the courtroom or the journalist's notebook. On Twitter/X, survivors speak directly — without institutional mediation, without a formal audience, and without the genre constraints of legal or journalistic discourse. The hashtag functions as an organizing structure that aggregates individual utterances into collective testimony, creating a corpus that is simultaneously personal and communal, anonymous and public, ephemeral and archived.

#WhyIDidntReport emerged in 2018 as a specific rhetorical response to the skepticism directed at survivors who delay reporting — a skepticism that was weaponized with particular visibility during the Brett Kavanaugh Supreme Court confirmation hearings. The hashtag created space for survivors to explain, on their own terms, the calculus of silence: the fear of not being believed, the threat of retaliation, the institutional failures that make reporting feel impossible or dangerous. Taken together, the #MeToo and #WhyIDidntReport corpora represent a form of collective counter-testimony — survivor language organized not around a specific case or institution, but around the shared experience of sexual violence and its aftermath.

Networked space is also where the counter-discourse is most active. The posts of figures like Andrew Tate — circulated by millions of followers, amplified by algorithmic recommendation, and explicitly framed as responses to #MeToo — represent the most direct confrontation between misogynistic suppressive rhetoric and survivor collective speech. Debbie Ging's research on the manosphere documents how this rhetoric functions as a coordinated effort to delegitimize survivor testimony at scale, not merely as opinion. Alice Marwick and Robyn Caplan have shown how this language spreads through network harassment, making the cost of speaking in digital survivor communities significant and concrete.

Corpus Analysis: Voyant Tools

Before applying sentence-level sentiment scoring, the full Twitter/X corpus was uploaded to Voyant Tools for exploratory analysis. Two visualizations proved particularly generative. The Constellation visualization, which uses a Word2Vec model to map semantic proximity between terms, reveals not just which words appear most often but how they cluster relationally: which concepts travel together, what associative structures organize survivor language at the level of meaning rather than mere frequency.

Close Reading: Selected Corpus Excerpts

The two posts below were selected through close reading of the corpus as representative examples of the rhetorical range within the #WhyIDidntReport dataset — one operating through layered, personal enumeration; the other through compressed, structural repetition. Together they illustrate how much affective and rhetorical complexity can be carried in a single tweet-length utterance.

#whyididntreport Fear of perp and consequences / Fear of police / Fear of being believed / Fear for children / Fear for animals / Fear of family / Just fear / Fear / And Fear / And humiliation #domesticviolence #Domesticabuse

#WhyIDidntReport · Twitter/X corpus

This post scored −0.9855 in VADER compound scoring — the most negatively affect-laden post in the entire corpus, sitting near the absolute floor of the scale. Its syntax is the reason. Where explanatory prose distributes negative weight across clauses, this post concentrates it through sheer repetition and fragmentation. Each line break is a new category of terror, and the accumulation strips language down until only the word itself remains: "Just fear / Fear / And Fear." VADER is highly sensitive to lexical density — a post that repeats a high-negativity word across multiple short units will score more severely than one that buries equivalent dread inside longer sentences. That syntactic fact is itself analytically significant: this post performs its fear structurally, not just semantically. The inclusion of fear for animals speaks to how thoroughly abuse colonizes a home — when violence is that pervasive, nothing within those walls is safe, not even the most vulnerable. The closing "And humiliation" sits entirely outside the fear structure, its placement suggesting something harder to categorize than fear: a wound that has no object except the self. It performs exhaustion instead of credibility.

#WhyIDidntReport Because I know how badly my character assassination would be given the circumstances that occurred before my assault. Because it hurts to relive it. Because I don't want my family to feel any pain or sadness.

#WhyIDidntReport · Twitter/X corpus

This post also scores among the most negatively affect-laden in the corpus — and yet its rhetorical character could not be more different. It operates in full sentences, the grammar of explanation rather than fragmentation. The tripled "Because" is a deliberate structure: each clause names a different category of harm that silence protects against. The first is social (character assassination), the second is psychological (the cost of memory), and the third is relational (family). Unlike the fear post, the negative weight here is distributed across complex syntax — longer clauses, embedded subordination, the grammar of someone constructing a careful argument rather than listing what they cannot hold. That VADER still scores it among the corpus's most negative reveals something important: the tool is reading the lexical content accurately, but the syntactic form — restrained, explanatory, protective — represents the kind of emotional labor VADER cannot measure. The score captures the weight. It cannot capture the logic of survival that chose these particular words to carry it.

Section VI — Supplement

Visual Rhetoric: Instagram & Threads

Data collection was attempted on Instagram using the same Zeeschuimer methodology applied to Twitter/X. However, Instagram content did not yield analyzable text — posts did not transfer coherently into Voyant Tools or the VADER sentiment pipeline. In the interest of methodological consistency, written Instagram content was excluded from computational analysis.

Instagram's answer to Twitter/X as a text-based platform increasingly lives on Threads, Meta's text-focused social network, which launched in 2023 and has grown substantially as a space for public discourse. Future work could productively pose the Threads content alongside the Twitter/X corpus collected here, allowing for a more complete picture of how survivor language circulates across competing networked text environments. Whether Threads produces the same patterns of collective testimony, solidarity language, and counter-discourse as Twitter/X is an open and generative research question.

The question of Instagram's visual register, however, remains vital and irreducible to text. Survivor advocacy on Instagram circulates significantly through imagery — infographics, typographic posts, photographs, and visual campaigns — that exceeds what any text-based sentiment tool can capture. Analyzing trends in visual imagery on platforms like Instagram requires multimodal methodologies that this project gestures toward but cannot yet fully execute. ATLAS Women

Acting Together: Law, Advice, Support. ATLAS empowers, supports, and connects women working in or entering careers in public international law. An international feminist organization working to advance gender justice through the International Criminal Court and domestic mechanisms across fifteen countries, including Ukraine, Afghanistan, Colombia, and Palestine. A UK-based charity offering trauma-informed, whole-person recovery services for survivors of abuse, domestic violence, and substance use across the South West of England and Wales. Section IX

Discussion & Limitations

While VADER represents a well-validated and widely used tool for computational sentiment analysis, its application to survivor testimony and trauma narrative requires careful qualification. VADER was trained primarily on Amazon product reviews, movie reviews, and Twitter data — registers that operate under a specific and largely unexamined affective economy. Amazon reviews in particular are characterized by a structural hyperbole: a product that works as expected tends to generate neutral to borderline positive responses, while a product that fails generates emphatic, amplified negative language. Dissatisfaction is expressed loudly; satisfaction is expressed flatly. The tool is calibrated to this asymmetry. Survivor language inverts it entirely. In legal testimony, survivors often flatten or suppress explicit emotional expression — not because the emotion is absent, but because the institutional context punishes it. Anger reads as instability. Grief reads as unreliability. The rhetorical restraint survivors adopt to be believed is, to VADER's training data, indistinguishable from neutrality. Conversely, when survivors do speak explicitly — in the networked space of a hashtag, where the audience is peers rather than judges — that directness may be read by the tool as operating in the same register as a one-star product review, collapsing the distance between "this blender broke" and "I was afraid to go to the police." The tool cannot distinguish between hyperbole deployed to emphasize consumer disappointment and the precise, unembellished language of someone describing an assault. Survivor language is either flattened for a specific audience or explicitly spoken — and VADER will confuse both modes with the emotional register it was built to recognize.

VADER may also misread the language of resilience and defiance. Words associated with strength and agency may score as positive in ways that flatten complexity, while the clinical language survivors sometimes adopt to describe acts of violence may register as neutral when the content is anything but. The compound score, by collapsing negative, neutral, and positive valences into a single number, necessarily loses information: a sentence that contains both grief and defiance in equal measure may score near zero not because it is emotionally empty, but because its competing valences cancel each other out.

These limitations underscore the importance of treating this project as what it claims to be: a baseline model. The computational findings presented here are most productively read alongside, rather than instead of, close reading and feminist theoretical interpretation. As Lauren Klein and Catherine D'Ignazio argue in Data Feminism, data is never a view from nowhere — it is always shaped by the choices, tools, and assumptions that produced it. Naming those constraints is a methodological and ethical responsibility, not a concession of failure.

Section X

Conclusion

Survivor language is not monolithic. It is shaped by audience, institution, and access. A victim impact statement and a #MeToo tweet may both be acts of testimony, but they are testimony under fundamentally different conditions — and those conditions leave traces in the language that computational methods can help make visible. This is the core claim of this baseline model, and the data examined here bears it out.

Understanding those shapes — computationally, linguistically, and feminist theoretically — is the first step toward building spaces where survivors do not have to translate themselves to be heard. Mapping the contours of survivor speech is not an end in itself. It is a way of asking: what would it look like to design legal institutions, journalistic practices, and digital platforms that did not require survivors to perform credibility in order to be believed? What would it look like to take the language seriously on its own terms?

The corpus answers that question in the words survivors actually chose. Two posts from the Twitter/X dataset surface here as a closing frame, selected because together they hold the full arc of what this project has tried to map: the demand, and the response to it.

No means No! Please stop the silence and say no to rape and rape culture #whyididntreport

#WhyIDidntReport · Twitter/X corpus

I have never felt the way I did when all those survivors shared their story!! Know that I am with you! I believe you! No matter what, I will stand with you! I am so PROUD OF YOUR COURAGE ♥️ #metoomv #whyididntreport #SurvivorsofRape

#MeToo · Twitter/X corpus

The first is a demand. The second is a covenant. Between them, they represent the two modes of survivor speech that the networked corpus holds in constant tension: the political and the relational, the indictment and the embrace. Sentiment analysis will assign each a score. What it cannot score is the relationship between them — the way one makes the other possible, the way testimony creates the conditions for solidarity. That is the work this project points toward, and the work that remains.

Addendum

Ethical Statement

The data examined in this project exists across a spectrum of public availability. The victim impact statements of Chanel Miller, Aly Raisman, Kyle Stephens, and Rachael Denhollander have been published, entered into the public legal record, and studied extensively in both journalistic and academic contexts. The CNN accounts were shared by survivors with full knowledge that they would be publicly facing. In both cases, survivors made deliberate choices to enter public discourse, and this project treats their language with the scholarly care that choice deserves.

The Twitter/X corpus presents a more complex ethical terrain. Although Twitter/X is a public platform, those who post — particularly under emotionally vulnerable circumstances — may not fully anticipate that their words could become research data. Awareness of platform publicity is not the same as informed consent to research participation, and this project takes that distinction seriously. No individual user is identified by name in this analysis. Quoted posts are used only where they illuminate broader patterns, not to expose individual people.

Users who post under the #MeToo hashtag are choosing to affiliate with a collective. The hashtag itself is an act of public solidarity, an explicit declaration of participation in a shared project of disclosure and cultural change. In using #MeToo, users signal their membership in a larger movement. This project understands itself as part of that same project, and is deeply grateful to every person who used their voice to contribute to it and to help create change.

The Instagram images included in this project were hand-collected from public accounts and are fully attributed. They are presented as visual evidence of survivor advocacy rhetoric, not as data extracted without acknowledgment.

A Note on AI-Assisted Tools

Claude.ai (Anthropic) was used at two stages of this project. During the sentiment analysis process, it was consulted to troubleshoot Python code — debugging errors in the VADER pipeline, the NLTK tokenization process, and the Matplotlib visualization scripts. It was also used to build and iterate on the HTML and CSS structure of this webpage. All analytical interpretation, close reading, theoretical framing, corpus selection, and scholarly argumentation are the author's own. The computational outputs — sentiment scores, rolling averages, word clouds — were generated by the author using the Python environment described in the methodology section. Claude.ai assisted with the technical scaffolding that made those outputs possible and with the presentation layer of this site. Naming this is a matter of scholarly transparency and intellectual honesty: the tools used to produce a project are part of its methodology, and AI assistance is no exception to that principle.

Looking Forward

Directions for Future Research

This project is explicitly a baseline — a first, rigorous attempt at a model that invites expansion, refinement, and critique. The following represent the most pressing directions identified through this research process.

01
Epstein Survivor Transcripts

The testimonies emerging from the Epstein case represent an essential and as-yet unanalyzed corpus for this model. Because the legal situation remains active and transcripts have not reached a stable, ethically appropriate form for computational analysis, they were excluded here. Their inclusion in future work is both imminent and necessary.

02
Local Victim Impact Statements — Tarrant County, TX

A significant open question raised by this project is whether the linguistic patterns identified in high-profile cases reflect survivor language more broadly, or whether visibility, legal representation, and media exposure shape testimony in ways that distinguish high-profile from local cases. This question is currently being explored in collaboration with a victim advocacy agent in Tarrant County, Texas, who is working to collect a sample of local victim impact statements for comparative analysis. This process requires careful ethical navigation and institutional approval, and could not be completed within the scope of this project. It represents one of the most meaningful directions for future work.

03
Threads as a Companion Corpus to Twitter/X

Instagram's answer to Twitter/X as a text-based networked platform is Threads, Meta's text-focused social network. Future work could productively compare Threads content alongside the Twitter/X corpus collected here — examining whether the same patterns of collective testimony, solidarity language, and counter-discourse emerge across competing platforms, and whether platform architecture and moderation policies produce measurable differences in survivor language.

04
Multimodal Analysis of Instagram Visual Culture

Survivor advocacy on Instagram circulates significantly through visual imagery — infographics, typographic posts, photographs, and campaigns — that exceeds what text-based sentiment tools can capture. Future work must develop or adapt multimodal methodologies capable of analyzing visual rhetoric alongside text, treating the images survivors and advocates share as data in their own right.

05
Trauma-Informed Sentiment Lexicons

The limitations of VADER for trauma and legal testimony corpora point toward a clear methodological gap: the field needs sentiment tools trained on or validated against survivor language specifically. Building a trauma-informed NLP lexicon represents a significant contribution this work gestures toward.

06
Intersectional Corpus Expansion

The current corpus reflects a particular slice of survivor visibility — cases that reached national media attention. Future work should deliberately incorporate testimony from communities whose voices are structurally less visible, including survivors of color, LGBTQ+ survivors, and survivors in non-Anglophone contexts.

Contact & Contribute

This project is a baseline — and baselines grow. If you have arrived here and recognize your own experience in what you have read, or if you work alongside survivors whose voices belong in this research, I want to hear from you.

I am actively seeking narratives for inclusion in future iterations of this corpus. Survivor language in all its forms — written testimony, social media posts, personal accounts — is the foundation this research is built on, and the model is only as complete as the voices it holds. I am especially seeking contributions from those whose experiences have been most systemically silenced: trans survivors, survivors of color, survivors whose stories do not fit the high-profile cases that tend to dominate public discourse, and survivors whose cultural or linguistic contexts have kept them outside English-language research frameworks entirely. If you would like to contribute your narrative for the purposes of this research, you retain full control over how it is used, attributed, or anonymized. No contribution is too small, and no story is too complicated.

I also welcome feedback and commentary on the project itself — its methodology, its framing, its gaps, and its arguments. This work is in conversation, not finished.
